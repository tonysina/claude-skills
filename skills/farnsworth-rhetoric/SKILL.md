# Farnsworth Rhetoric: Memorable Writing

Apply classical rhetorical techniques to strengthen writing impact.

## Task

When strengthening writing:

1. Identify the key message or emotional peak
2. Work backward from endings—they matter most
3. Apply techniques below where they fit naturally
4. Use sparingly—these devices call attention to themselves
5. Preserve meaning; add craft

## Master Principle

**Contrast creates impact.** Every technique works best when set against its opposite. The ear detects differences.

---

## Technique 1: Saxon vs. Latinate Words

English has two vocabularies:

| Saxon (Germanic) | Latinate (French/Latin) |
|------------------|------------------------|
| make | create |
| get | acquire |
| let | permit |
| kill | terminate |
| hate | enmity |

**Saxon:** Short, hard sounds. Hit the gut. No mental translation needed.
**Latinate:** Longer, softer. Appeal to intellect. Create distance.

### Default

Prefer Saxon. When you really know what you're talking about, you can say it simply.

### Better

Mix for contrast. Use Latinate to set up Saxon.

Before: "The organization's strategic imperatives necessitate immediate implementation."
After: "We need to act now."

---

## Technique 2: Saxon Finish

End sentences and paragraphs with simple Saxon words, especially after Latinate buildup.

**Pattern:** Complex → simple. Abstract → concrete. The last word rings longest.

Before: "This policy promotes organizational transformation."
After: "This policy will change how we work."

**Strong ending words:** work, fight, stand, fall, hate, love, live, die, win, lose, end, start, break, build

Before: "...freedom for the thought that we hate." (Holmes)
The word "hate" is the punch.

---

## Technique 3: Say It Twice

State the idea in Latinate (conceptual), then restate in Saxon (visceral).

Lincoln: "I do not expect the union to be dissolved. I do not expect the house to fall."

Same idea twice:
- "Union dissolved" = abstract, political
- "House fall" = visual, biblical

**When to use:** Key points that need both intellectual and emotional resonance.

Before: "Digital transformation requires organizational change management."
After: "Digital transformation requires organizational change management. Put simply: everything will change."

---

## Technique 4: Anaphora

Repeat words at the beginning of consecutive clauses.

"We shall fight on the beaches. We shall fight on the landing grounds. We shall fight in the fields."

**Effect:** Captures ear, creates rising anticipation, drives home the repeated phrase.

**When to use:** Building intensity, emphasizing commitment, creating rhythm in calls to action.

Before: "The team needs to focus on quality. They should also prioritize speed. And they must maintain cost discipline."
After: "We must focus on quality. We must prioritize speed. We must maintain cost discipline."

---

## Technique 5: Epistrophe

Repeat words at the end of consecutive clauses.

"Government of the people, by the people, for the people."

**Effect:** The repeated word rings in the ear at the most memorable position.

**When to use:** Conclusions, key themes, messages you want retained.

Before: "This approach serves our customers, benefits our employees, and helps our shareholders."
After: "This is how we serve. This is why we build. This is what we do."

---

## Technique 6: Mix Anaphora and Epistrophe

Shift from one to the other for contrast.

Churchill: "giving all, daring all, enduring all to the utmost, to the end."

Epistrophe (all, all, all) → Anaphora (to the..., to the...)

The shift refreshes the ear.

---

## Technique 7: Chiasmus

A-B-B-A reversal structure.

"Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country."

Structure: country → you → you → country

### When to use (test these triggers)

1. **"They have it backwards"** — Relationship is reversed
2. **"And vice versa"** — Reciprocal situation
3. **"It's the other way around"** — Mismatch to correct

Before: "Don't let work define your life. Make sure your life shapes your work."
After: "Don't let work define your life—let your life define your work."

**Caution:** Only certain statements lend themselves to chiasmus. Don't force it.

---

## Technique 8: Rule of Three

Three repetitions create rhythm and resolution. Two feels incomplete. Four feels excessive.

Before: "We value innovation, collaboration, integrity, excellence, and growth."
After: "We value innovation, integrity, and results."

The third item carries the weight of resolution.

---

## Technique 9: Strategic Passive Voice

Don't avoid passive voice reflexively. Use it when it creates better contrast or rhythm.

Active: "Never have so many owed so much to so few."
Passive: "Never was so much owed by so many to so few."

The passive puts "so many to so few" adjacent. The contrast lands harder.

**Rule:** Have a good reason. Know why you're making each choice.

---

## Application Guide

### High-impact contexts (use multiple techniques)

- Speeches
- Executive summaries
- Key messages / taglines
- Thought leadership conclusions
- Calls to action

### Light-touch contexts (use 1-2 techniques)

- Email subject lines
- Presentation headlines
- Meeting agendas
- Blog post hooks

### Avoid in

- Technical documentation
- Process instructions
- Data reporting
- Legal language

---

## Quick Revision Checklist

1. **Check endings.** Does each paragraph finish with a strong Saxon word?
2. **Spot Latinate pileups.** Break them with simple words or a Saxon finish.
3. **Find the key point.** Could you state it twice—abstract then concrete?
4. **Test for chiasmus.** Are you saying "they've got it backwards"? Try reversal.
5. **Count repetitions.** Is there a natural three-part structure?
6. **Verify contrast.** Does each technique have something to push against?

## Full Example

**Before:**
> The implementation of our customer experience transformation initiative will require significant organizational commitment and cross-functional collaboration. This strategic priority represents an opportunity to enhance our competitive positioning and drive sustainable growth.

**After:**
> This transformation will test us. It will demand commitment. It will require every team to work together. But if we commit, if we collaborate, if we follow through—we will not just compete. We will lead.

**Techniques applied:**
- Saxon finish ("will test us", "work together", "we will lead")
- Anaphora ("It will...", "if we...")
- Rule of three ("commit, collaborate, follow through")
- Say it twice ("compete" → "lead" restates the payoff simply)
- Contrast (Latinate setup in original → Saxon throughout revision)
