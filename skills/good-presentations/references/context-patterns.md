# Context-Specific Deck Patterns

Three contexts, three structural patterns. The rhetoric principles in SKILL.md
apply to all three. These patterns specify what each context demands on top
of those principles.

---

## Webinar / Live Presentation

The audience is present. Attention is contested — phones, email, side
conversations. You cannot assume continuous engagement. Build in reorientation.

### Structural pattern

1. **Opening provocation** — surprising stat, puzzle, or bold claim. No agenda.
2. **Stakes slide** — why this matters to this audience specifically
3. **Argument preview** — one slide stating your conclusion upfront (pyramid)
4. **Act II: Development** — evidence and analysis, 3-5 slides per major point
5. **Transition slides** — dark background, reorient between major sections
6. **Devil's Advocate** — strongest objection + your response
7. **Closing takeaway** — one sentence, no summary, no "Questions?"

### Rules specific to webinars

- Slides support speech, not replace it. If a slide can be fully understood
  without you speaking, it's a document pretending to be a slide.
- Embed reorientation: "Remember the gap we opened with? Here's where it
  becomes concrete." This pulls back distracted audience members without
  condescending.
- Each section transition should briefly reconstruct context: "We've seen X.
  Now let's look at why that matters for Y."
- Speaker notes should carry elaboration the slide deliberately omits.

### What to avoid

- Agenda slides with 6-8 items (overwhelming and forgettable)
- Dense text slides read verbatim
- Closing on a generic "Thank you / Questions?" slide
- Building an argument step-by-step and withholding the conclusion until the end

## Thought Leadership

These decks circulate independently. Someone may read them six months after
you created them, without context, without you present. Every slide must
carry meaning alone.

### Structural pattern

1. **Title slide** — clear, specific, promises a point of view
2. **The central claim** — your argument in one sentence, stated immediately
3. **Why now / why this matters** — stakes for this audience at this moment
4. **Evidence** — 3-5 slides, each with an assertion title and direct data
5. **Implication 1** — what this means for practice/strategy/thinking
6. **Implication 2** — if applicable
7. **Devil's Advocate** — strongest objection + response (builds credibility
   for readers who encounter this skeptically)
8. **Closing** — one memorable sentence that crystallizes the argument

### Rules specific to thought leadership

- Titles carry the entire argument. A reader who skims only titles must
  come away with the complete point of view.
- Every figure must be self-interpreting: title states the finding, data is
  labeled directly, source is noted. No legends requiring cross-reference.
- Acknowledge what this argument does NOT claim. Overclaiming destroys
  credibility with sophisticated readers.
- The deck should have a distinct point of view — not "here are some
  considerations" but "here is what we believe and why."
- Footnotes and source notes matter more here than in live presentations.
  Later readers want to verify.

### What to avoid

- False balance ("on one hand... on the other hand...") without a conclusion
- Bullet lists of "key takeaways" as the closing slide
- Charts without titles that state the finding
- Avoiding the Devil's Advocate because it feels risky — it builds credibility

## Knowledge Summary / Reference Material

Denser than live presentations. The audience will consult this rather than
experience it. But it is still argued — not just a data dump.

### Structural pattern

1. **Title + scope** — what this covers and what it doesn't
2. **Central argument** — even a reference doc has a main point; state it
3. **Sections by theme or chronology** — each with a transition slide
4. **Within each section**: assertion titles, one idea per slide, evidence
5. **Synthesis slide** — how the pieces connect to the central argument
6. **Limitations / what this doesn't cover** — honest scope boundaries
7. **References or further reading** — if applicable

### Rules specific to knowledge summaries

- Density is acceptable here — more content per slide than a live deck.
  But each slide still has one idea. Density means more evidence per idea,
  not multiple ideas stacked.
- Section structure must be explicit. Use clear headers and transition slides.
  Readers will navigate non-linearly.
- The document-like nature does NOT mean abandoning assertion titles.
  "Overview of Research Methods" is a label. "Self-report bias limits most
  survey-based studies in this field" is a title.
- Include a synthesis slide that connects the pieces to the central argument.
  Reference docs that never answer "so what?" fail their audience.
- Formatting consistency matters more here than in live decks — readers
  scan structure when navigating.

### What to avoid

- Treating this as a filing cabinet rather than an argument
- Section headers as the only organizational signal (use assertion titles
  within sections too)
- Omitting the "so what" — even reference material should answer why it matters
- Overcrowding because "it's just reference" — white space still aids
  navigation and comprehension

## The Dual Audience Problem

Thought leadership and knowledge summaries increasingly reach audiences
beyond their original context. A webinar deck gets forwarded. A summary
circulates without its creator.

Strategies that help all three contexts:

- **Assertion titles** carry the argument for readers who only skim
- **Self-interpreting figures** work without the speaker
- **Speaker notes** provide elaboration for live decks without cluttering slides
- **The Devil's Advocate slide** preempts skepticism from readers who
  encounter the material cold
