# Structural Models Reference

This file provides the core psychological frameworks for strategic messaging design.

---

## Why These Models Work

Effective persuasion targets systems deeper than conscious thought. The brain processes influence through four layers:

1. **Impulse** (deepest — ancestral scripts in the brain stem)
2. **Emotion** (neurochemical cascades that override rational processing)
3. **Behavioral patterns** (learned scripts from repetition and experience)
4. **Thoughts** (conscious reasoning — the weakest lever)

Most persuasion targets only thoughts. These models target all four layers, which is why they produce disproportionate results.

### Ancestral vs. Life Scripts

**Ancestral Scripts** are hard-wired behavioral patterns inherited through DNA. They activate automatically in response to FATE triggers (Focus, Authority, Tribe, Emotion) and can override rational decision-making entirely. Example: the fight-or-flight response to a perceived threat.

**Life Scripts** are learned behavioral patterns built through repetition and experience. They save cognitive energy by automating responses to familiar situations. Example: how someone responds to conflict based on what worked in childhood.

**Four Rules of Behavioral Scripts:**
1. If a script is **interrupted**, focus is created
2. If a script is **borrowed from someone's past experience**, predictability is created
3. If a script is **borrowed from ancestors**, automation is created
4. If a script is **openly discussed**, its power is lessened

Use rule 1 for attention capture (pattern interrupts). Use rule 2 to create familiarity and comfort. Use rule 3 to trigger automatic compliance. Avoid rule 4 — never name the technique you're using.

## 1. FATE Model (Mammalian/Ancestral Triggers)

The FATE model leverages primal drives that bypass rational thinking. These are the four "entry points" into the human mind — ordered by importance to ancestral survival.

### Framework Table

| Factor | Why It Works | Execution Guidelines |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Focus** | Ancestors who couldn't focus on threats and resources died. Our brains are pre-wired to fixate on novelty and potential danger. | Inject novelty and unexpected statements; avoid filter words like "maybe," "perhaps," "might"; use pattern interrupts |
| **Authority** | Disobeying the tribal leader meant banishment or death. The presence of authority triggers an ancestral script that overrides personal judgment. | Use authoritative language; quote recognized experts (third-party authority); speak with certainty; leverage the Authority Triangle |
| **Tribe** | Survival required group belonging. Being cast out meant death. Our brains automatically monitor group behavior and enforce conformity. | Use statistics or generalize acceptance; cite peer behavior; activate the six elements of the "tribe script" |
| **Emotion** | Neurochemical reactions create automatic reminder systems (approach or avoid). Emotions served as survival data for ancestors. | Use vivid, sensory language; activate security scripts (risk aversion) or deviance scripts (opportunity seeking) |

### Focus Techniques

**Attention-Grabbing Openings:**
- Start with unexpected statistics
- Open with provocative questions
- Use pattern interrupts (surprising statements that break an expected script — rule 1)
- Create mystery or information gaps (curiosity activates the brain's reward-seeking system)

**Examples:**
- "97% of people who wait regret it. Here's why you won't be one of them."
- "What if everything you believed about this decision was backwards?"
- "Three things just changed that make this urgent."

### Authority Application

**The Authority Triangle** (three dimensions that establish authority):
- **Behavior**: Confidence, discipline, leadership, gratitude — the internal traits that project authority
- **Effect**: What others unconsciously perceive — movement, appearance, confidence, connection, intent
- **Habits**: Lifestyle factors that either reinforce or undermine authority perception

In writing, authority is established through certainty of language, reference to credible sources, and framing yourself as a peer-level advisor rather than a supplicant.

**Direct Authority:**
- "Research from [prestigious institution] confirms..."
- "Industry leaders at [known companies] have already moved on this."
- "The data unequivocally shows..."

**Third-Party Authority:**
- "According to [expert name], the window for this is closing."
- "[Authoritative source] published findings that validate this approach."
- "Every major player in this space has concluded..."

### Tribe Activation

The ancestral "tribe script" has six automatic components. Persuasive writing can activate any of them:

1. Pay attention to the group
2. Keep personal behavior in line with the group
3. Default to group behavior instead of personal desires
4. Enforce group behavior
5. Socially enforce compliance with group norms
6. Continuously ensure personal behavior won't cause social consequences

**Social Proof Patterns:**
- "[X%] of people in your position have already..."
- "Everyone from [respected group] is moving toward this."
- "The early majority has already made this shift."
- "Your peers at [comparable organizations] have adopted this."

**Conformity Triggers:**
- "This is becoming the new standard."
- "Those who haven't moved are being left behind."
- "The consensus is clear among thought leaders."

### Emotion Triggering

Emotions create neurochemical cascades that serve as behavioral data. The brain treats the *threat* of resource loss nearly identically to *actual* resource loss.

**Security Scripts (Risk Aversion):**
- "Protect what you've built by..."
- "Secure your position before..."
- "Guard against the risk of..."
- Paint vivid pictures of loss scenarios

**Deviance Scripts (Opportunity Seeking):**
- "Break free from constraints by..."
- "Seize advantage while others sleep..."
- "Capture the upside that conformists miss..."
- Paint vivid pictures of winning scenarios

## 2. PCP Model (Compliance Hierarchy)

The PCP model is the foundation of all influence. It contains the raw elements of how persuasion starts, develops, and ends. Any situation should first be analyzed through PCP before applying the Six-Axis Model and FATE.

The sequence is critical: **Perception must shift before Context can be established, and Context must be established before Permission is effective.** Skipping steps causes resistance.

### Framework Table

| Phase | Goal | Why This Sequence | Execution Guidelines |
|-------|------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Perception** | Shift how the subject views the problem/opportunity | People interpret new information through existing frames. Change the frame first, or everything else bounces off. | Challenge assumptions using emotional triggers and authoritative fact placement; reframe the situation |
| **Context** | Make the desired action socially acceptable | Once perception shifts, the subject needs to see the new behavior as normal within a reference group they identify with. | Frame the action within a context where the behavior is standard; provide social proof and permission structures |
| **Permission** | Grant the subject the license to act without guilt | Even when they see differently and know it's normal, people need psychological permission to overcome inertia and self-doubt. | Use dissociative language or project blame onto external factors; remove barriers to action |

### Perception Phase

**Goal:** Change how they see the situation before asking for action.

**Techniques:**
- Present data that contradicts their current view
- Use authority figures to validate the new perspective
- Employ emotional triggers to create urgency
- Reframe the problem/opportunity

**Examples:**
- "What you've been told about [topic] is incomplete. Here's what the data actually shows..."
- "The traditional view no longer applies because [major change]."
- "Your current framework misses three critical factors..."

### Context Phase

**Goal:** Make the action feel normal and socially acceptable.

**Techniques:**
- Shift to contexts where the behavior is standard
- Use social proof to normalize the action
- Compare to familiar, accepted behaviors
- Create reference groups where this is the norm

**Context Shifting** (reframe the category of the interaction):
- "Sales" → "Advisory" (you're not selling, you're advising)
- "Spending" → "Investing" (it's not a cost, it's an investment)
- "Risk" → "Opportunity" (it's not dangerous, it's advantageous)
- "Change" → "Evolution" (it's not disruption, it's natural progression)

**Examples:**
- "In high-performing organizations, this is standard practice."
- "Think of this like [familiar situation] where everyone does [similar action]."
- "Your peers at [company/institution] treat this as routine."

### Permission Phase

**Goal:** Remove psychological barriers and guilt about taking action.

**Techniques:**
- Externalize responsibility ("Market conditions require...")
- Blame external factors, not the subject
- Frame action as response to circumstances beyond control
- Give explicit permission statements

**Examples:**
- "It's not your fault the timing wasn't right before. Now it is."
- "The circumstances have changed. This is no longer optional."
- "You're responding to forces beyond anyone's control."
- "Everyone in your position would make the same choice."

**Permission Statements:**
- "You have every right to..."
- "It's completely acceptable to..."
- "No one would blame you for..."
- "This is exactly what you should do."

## 3. Six-Axis Model (6AM)

The Six-Axis Model defines precisely which six psychological states must be present for influence to occur. Only three need to be strongly present, but they must be at high levels. All six are modifiable and fluid.

### Framework Table

| State | Definition | Key Linguistic Tools |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Focus** | Degree to which a person maintains attention and excludes competing stimuli | Attention-piquing phrases; novelty; pattern interrupts; eliminate distractions |
| **Openness** | Degree to which a person feels they can become completely vulnerable, without fear of social repercussions | Consistency-hacking compliments ("You're so open"); dissociation; create safety |
| **Suggestibility** | Degree to which a person will accept and then act on a given suggestion | Confusion techniques; regression language; embed commands |
| **Compliance** | Degree to which a person will comply with a direction to perform an action | Presuppositions; double binds; incremental commitments |
| **Expectancy** | Degree to which a person reasonably feels they can predict what's to come, and that it will be enjoyed | Future-pacing; scarcity (fear of losing reward); visualize success |
| **Connection** | Degree to which a person feels a sense of social connection marked by desire for future contact | Shared universal experiences; controlled vulnerability; match communication style |

### Prioritizing States

When the behavior profile indicates priority states (max 4), allocate more linguistic attention to those states:

- **High Priority States:** Dedicate 2-3 specific techniques per state
- **Medium Priority States:** Include 1-2 techniques per state
- **Low Priority States:** Background presence only

Priority selection depends on the situation. General patterns:

| Situation Type | Typical Priority Order |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Sales (in-person) | Expectancy, Focus, Suggestibility, Compliance, Connection, Openness |
| Sales (door-to-door/cold) | Focus, Connection, Expectancy, Openness, Suggestibility, Compliance |
| Investor pitch | Focus, Expectancy, Connection, Suggestibility, Compliance, Openness |
| Change management | Openness, Connection, Expectancy, Compliance, Focus, Suggestibility |
| Job negotiation | Focus, Connection, Expectancy, Openness, Compliance, Suggestibility |
| Therapy/mentoring | Openness, Suggestibility, Expectancy, Compliance, Focus, Connection |
| Time-limited situations | Focus typically leads (you must be memorable before you can be persuasive) |

### State Techniques

**Focus:** Open with unexpected information; use sensory language; create information gaps; avoid filler; break patterns with surprise.
> "Stop. Before you make any decision, three things changed yesterday that you need to know."

**Openness:** Compliment their openness; share controlled vulnerability; create safety; use consistency traps (get agreement on being "open-minded," then link to hearing your message).
> "You strike me as someone who's genuinely open to looking at things from different angles. That's rare and valuable."

**Suggestibility:** Use confusion to bypass critical thinking; employ regression language; embed commands within complex sentences; create ambiguity that requires acceptance of your frame.
> "As you think about what I'm saying, you might notice that you're already beginning to see how this makes sense."

**Compliance:** Use presuppositions ("When you move forward..."); employ double binds ("Will you start tomorrow or next week?"); get small commitments first; use "yes sets."
> "You can decide to move forward now, or you can schedule time next week to finalize everything. Which works better?"

**Expectancy:** Future-pace success; create scarcity; visualize specific benefits; link action to rewarding outcomes.
> "Picture yourself three months from now, looking back on today as the moment everything changed."

**Connection:** Reference shared experiences; show controlled vulnerability; match communication style (visual/auditory/kinesthetic); use "we" language.
> "I get it. I've been exactly where you are — staring at a decision that felt too big. Here's what changed everything for me."

## 4. The Behavior Compass (Needs + Decisions + Values)

### Needs Map

Social needs operate like chemical addictions. When a person's primary Need is met, neuropeptides flood through the body and attach to receptor sites on cells. Over time, cells multiply receptor sites, increasing demand for that specific chemical. When the need isn't met, the body literally screams at the brain to manufacture a scenario that produces the chemicals.

This is why people seem compulsively driven to repeat the same social patterns — they are chemically addicted to the confirmation of their primary Need.

| Need | What They Crave | Hidden Fears | Linguistic Approach |
|------|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Significance** | To matter, stand out, be important | Abandonment, social ridicule, being ignored, feeling small | "Leaders like you..." / "This is what sets you apart..." |
| **Approval** | To be validated, acknowledged, respected | Dismissal, disapproval, contempt, feeling left out | "Everyone respects people who..." / "You'll be recognized for..." |
| **Acceptance** | To belong, fit in, be part of a group | Social criticism, gossip, peer mismatch | "You belong with the group that..." / "People like us..." |
| **Intelligence** | To be smart, analytical, to understand | Being seen as dumb, being questioned, being "called out" | "Smart people like you..." / "Your intelligence tells you..." |
| **Pity** | Sympathy, to be seen as deserving help | Being disregarded, ignored, misunderstood | "After everything you've been through..." / "You deserve..." |
| **Strength/Power** | Control, dominance, authority | Being disrespected, challenged, "punked" | "Take control by..." / "Powerful people don't hesitate..." |

When you profile Needs, you're seeing what was missing during someone's formative years. The Need is the wound; the behavior is the self-medication.

### Decision Map

The Decision Map reveals HOW someone makes choices. Decision styles are interrelated — people usually share two adjacent styles.

| Style | Core Question | In Writing |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Deviance** | "Will this help me stand out or break norms?" | Frame as rebellion, differentiation, rule-breaking |
| **Novelty** | "Is this noticeably new and will others see it?" | Emphasize first-mover advantage, cutting-edge positioning |
| **Social** | "Will this make people show interest or connect with me?" | Emphasize social reactions, how others will respond |
| **Conformity** | "Does this match what respected people do?" | Emphasize tradition, standards, what established leaders do |
| **Investment** | "Will this provide a valuable return?" | Emphasize ROI, data, cost-benefit analysis |
| **Necessity** | "What specifically makes this necessary vs. other options?" | Emphasize urgency, requirements, elimination of alternatives |

### Values Map

The Values Map reveals WHY someone's Needs and Decisions exist — what they currently lack and strive toward. Values are typically rooted in what was missing during formative years and remains aspirational.

| Value | What They Pursue | How It Manifests |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Connection** | Deep relationships, belonging | "At the end of the day, what truly matters is family and friends." |
| **Adventure** | Novel experiences, excitement | "Life is too short to play it safe." |
| **Power** | Control, influence, authority | "If I got that promotion, everything would change." |
| **Freedom** | Autonomy, independence | "I just want to do things my own way." |
| **Stability** | Security, predictability | "Once things settle down, I can finally relax." |
| **Achievement** | Accomplishment, mastery | "I won't stop until I've done something that matters." |

**The Compass Flow:** Need (what they're addicted to getting) → Decision (how they choose to get it) → Value (what they believe having it will ultimately bring them)

## 5. Integration Guidelines

### Layering Models — Optimal Sequence

1. Open with **FATE** (Focus + Emotion) to capture attention and trigger ancestral scripts
2. Apply **PCP** (Perception) to shift their viewpoint
3. Activate **6AM** states (Openness, Suggestibility)
4. Build with **FATE** (Authority + Tribe) for social proof
5. Apply **PCP** (Context) to normalize the action
6. Trigger **6AM** states (Expectancy, Compliance)
7. Close with **PCP** (Permission) and **FATE** (Emotion)

### Model Selection by Task

| Task | Primary Models | Priority States |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| Quick decisions | FATE + 6AM | Focus, Compliance, Expectancy |
| Complex transformations | Full PCP + 6AM | Openness, Suggestibility |
| High-trust situations | FATE (Authority, Tribe) + 6AM | Connection, Expectancy |
| Resistant subjects | Heavy PCP + Dissociative linguistics | Perception, Context, Permission |
| Relationship-dependent | 6AM (Connection) + FATE (Emotion) | Connection, Openness |
