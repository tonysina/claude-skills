# Book Examples for Internal Guidance

These examples demonstrate Golden Circle principles. Use them to understand the approach, but do not cite them directly in outputs.

## Apple

**WHY:** Challenge the status quo, empower the individual, think differently

**Origin:** Jobs and Wozniak came of age in 1960s-70s Northern California where antigovernment ideals ran high. They saw technology as a way to give individuals power to stand up against established institutions.

**HOW:**
- Make products beautifully designed and simple to use
- Champion the creative spirit of the individual
- Challenge industry conventions

**WHAT:**
- Computers, phones, music players, services
- "1984" commercial -- a rebel smashing the status quo
- "Think Different" campaign -- individuals who changed the world
- Always individuals in advertising, never groups

**Communication Example:**

Outside-In (how most companies do it):
> We make great computers. They're beautifully designed, simple to use and user-friendly. Want to buy one?

Inside-Out (how Apple does it):
> Everything we do, we believe in challenging the status quo. We believe in thinking differently. The way we challenge the status quo is by making our products beautifully designed, simple to use and user-friendly. And we happen to make great computers.

**Why It Works:**
- Products prove beliefs; beliefs don't exist to sell products
- People don't buy what Apple makes -- they buy why Apple makes it
- This is why Apple can sell computers, phones, music, services -- all feel authentic

**Contrast: Dell**
- Dell has no clear WHY
- When Dell made mp3 players and PDAs, they felt inauthentic
- Without knowing what Dell believes, we can't see their products as anything more than commodities

## Southwest Airlines

**WHY:** Champion the common man, freedom for all

**Origin:** In early 1970s, only 15% of Americans traveled by air. Southwest didn't compete for that 15% -- they wanted to serve the other 85%. "We're the champion for the common man."

**HOW:**
- Cheap (affordable to average people)
- Fun (casual, not elitist)
- Simple (originally just two price categories)
- Employees first (customers second, shareholders third)

**WHAT:**
- Low-cost flights
- Open seating (no class structure -- fits their belief in equality)
- Fun flight attendants (originally hired cheerleaders and majorettes)
- "You are now free to move about the country"

**Hiring Insight:**
Southwest discovered that cheerleaders and majorettes made perfect flight attendants. Not because of their skills, but because their disposition aligned with the WHY -- spreading optimism, cheering people on.

Great companies don't hire skilled people and motivate them; they hire already motivated people and inspire them.

**Loyalty Example:**
After September 11, customers sent checks to Southwest to show support. One note with a $1,000 check read: "You've been so good to me over the years, in these hard times I wanted to say thank you by helping you out."

**Contrast: Ted and Song**
United's Ted and Delta's Song copied Southwest's WHAT (cheap, fun, simple) but failed within 4 years. Without a clear WHY, they were just commodities competing on price.

## Martin Luther King Jr.

**WHY:** Equality for all people, a belief that transcended civil rights for one group

**HOW:**
- Non-violent resistance
- Articulating beliefs clearly and repeatedly
- Building structure through organized movements

**WHAT:**
- Speeches, marches, protests
- "I Have a Dream" speech (not "I Have a Plan" speech)

**Key Insight:**
250,000 people showed up to hear King speak. No invitations were sent. No website to check. How?

They didn't show up FOR King. They showed up for themselves. King's WHY aligned with their WHY. Being there was one of their WHATs -- a way to prove what they believed.

**Why It Worked:**
- King gave people a place to go, not a plan to follow
- He talked about what he believed, not what to do
- Plans have their place, but not on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial
- Nearly a quarter of attendees were white -- this was about a shared belief in America

**The WHY/HOW Partnership:**
King was the WHY type (the dream). Ralph Abernathy was the HOW type (the plan). After King's rousing addresses, Abernathy would say: "Now let me tell you what that means for tomorrow morning."

## Harley-Davidson

**WHY:** Freedom, rebellion, individualism

**HOW:**
- Design bikes that embody the rebellious spirit
- Create a culture and community around the lifestyle

**WHAT:**
- Motorcycles
- Merchandise (12% of revenues from non-bike products)
- Tattoos -- customers literally brand themselves

**Why It Works:**
The Harley logo has meaning because the company has clarity, discipline, and consistency. When someone walks into a bar with a Harley tattoo, we know what they believe without them saying a word. The logo stopped being a corporate mark and became a symbol of identity.

**Symbols Insight:**
People tattoo Harley's logo on their bodies not because they love the corporation, but because the symbol represents who they are. The meaning belongs to the believer, not the brand. This is what happens when WHY is clear and consistent over time -- logos become symbols, and symbols become identity.

## Disney

**WHY:** Promote good, clean family fun

**Celery Test Example:**
A father has two DVDs -- one from an unknown company, one from Disney. Both are cartoons, both age-appropriate. Which does he put on for his child without watching first?

Disney. Because we trust what they believe. They've been so consistent that we know what to expect. This trust wasn't built through quality alone -- it was built through clarity of WHY.

## The Wright Brothers

**WHY:** Change the course of the world, prove human flight was possible

**Context:**
Samuel Pierpont Langley had funding, press attention, and the best minds. The Wright brothers had neither. Langley worked for fame and fortune. The Wrights worked for a cause.

**Result:**
When Orville achieved flight, Langley quit the same day -- his goal was to be first, not to change the world. The Wrights continued working on flight technology.

**Lesson:**
Those motivated by external factors (money, fame, being first) stop when obstacles arise or goals are achieved. Those driven by belief persist regardless. This is the difference between achievement motivation and purpose motivation.

## TiVo (Failure Example)

**What happened:** TiVo had the best product, became a household name, and had massive PR coverage. They seemed to have everything needed for success. But they were a commercial and financial failure.

**Why it failed:** TiVo marketed from the outside in. They led with WHAT: features, specifications, the ability to pause live TV, skip commercials, rewind. They told people what the product did and how it could improve their lives.

**What they should have done:** Started with WHY. If TiVo had launched with something like "If you're the kind of person who likes to have total control over every aspect of your life, boy do we have a product for you," they would have spoken to the left side of the bell curve -- people who identified with that belief. The features would have served as proof of the WHY, not the headline.

**Lesson for content:** Even the best product fails when you lead with WHAT. This is why thought leadership content must open with belief, not features. TiVo also ignored the Law of Diffusion -- they marketed to the mass market instead of winning believers first.

## Wal-Mart (WHY Goes Fuzzy Example)

**Sam Walton's WHY:** Serve the common man. If he looked after people, people would look after him. Service was the higher cause -- Wal-Mart was the vehicle.

**What went right:** Walton built a company where the WHY was felt everywhere. Employees were called "associates." Communities welcomed the stores. Low prices weren't a tactic -- they were proof of the belief that everyone deserves access.

**What went wrong:** After Walton's death, the company gradually confused WHY it existed (to serve people) with HOW it did business (to offer low prices). "Cheap" became the identity instead of "service." Efficiency and margins replaced the founding purpose.

**The result:** Lawsuits over labor practices, communities fighting to block new stores, employee morale issues. The WHAT and HOW didn't change, but the WHY went fuzzy. Outsiders could feel the shift even if they couldn't articulate it.

**Lesson for content:** This is the most common pattern in mature organizations. When WHY goes fuzzy, messaging becomes about features, price, and competitive positioning. If a user's content sounds like it could belong to any competitor, the WHY has probably gone fuzzy. The fix isn't better copywriting -- it's rediscovering the original belief.

## Achievement vs. Success

**The concept:** Achievement is something you reach or attain -- a goal, a number, a milestone. Success is a feeling, a state of being. You can achieve without feeling successful.

**The Gathering of Titans:** Every year, high-performing entrepreneurs meet at MIT. When asked how many had achieved their financial goals, 80% raised their hands. When asked how many felt successful, 80% of those hands went down. These multimillionaires longed for the feeling they had when working out of their basements.

**What happened:** In the course of building their businesses, they forgot WHY they started. They could measure what they'd gained (money, employees, market share) but not what they'd lost (purpose, meaning, the spark).

**Writing application:** This concept is powerful raw material for thought leadership aimed at executives and entrepreneurs. Many leaders feel this tension between achievement and success. Content that names this feeling and connects it to purpose can be deeply resonant -- it makes readers feel seen.

**The key distinction:** Achievement comes when you pursue and attain WHAT you want. Success comes when you are clear in pursuit of WHY you want it. Achievements serve as milestones indicating you're on the right path, but they are not the destination.

## The Celery Test

**Concept:**
Imagine getting advice at a party: "You need M&Ms!" "You need rice milk!" "You need Oreo cookies!" "You need celery!"

Without a WHY, you buy everything -- expensive, time-consuming, and nobody can see what you believe when you're standing in the checkout line.

With a clear WHY (healthy living), you buy only rice milk and celery. Less time, less money, and everyone can see what you believe.

**Application:**
- Filter all decisions through your WHY
- If a WHAT doesn't fit the WHY, it fails the test
- When WHY is clear, decisions become obvious
- Consistency builds trust over time

**VW Example (Failure):**
Volkswagen ("people's car") launched the $70,000 Phaeton luxury car. Despite excellent engineering, few bought it. A luxury car didn't fit what VW believes. Toyota and Honda created separate brands (Lexus, Acura) for luxury -- they understood the Celery Test.
