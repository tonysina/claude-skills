# Start with Why Writing Skill

Create inspiring thought leadership content using the Golden Circle framework from Simon Sinek's "Start with Why."

## Core Principle

People don't buy WHAT you do, they buy WHY you do it. Inspiring content communicates from the inside out: WHY -> HOW -> WHAT. This works because the limbic brain (where decisions and loyalty originate) responds to belief and belonging, not to features and facts. The neocortex handles rational analysis, but the limbic brain drives behavior.

## Workflow

### 1. Gather Context

Collect from user:
- Content type (article, speech, presentation, post, etc.)
- Intended outlet/platform and desired length
- Target audience (default: business professionals)
- Topic or subject matter

### 2. Assess Starting Point

Not every user needs full WHY discovery. Branch based on where they are:

**Path A -- WHY already clear:** If the user provides a purpose, cause, or belief that passes the validation checklist below, skip to Step 3. Don't force discovery on someone who's already there.

**Path B -- WHY needs discovery:** If the user leads with products, features, market position, or goals (money, growth, being #1), they need discovery. Go to Step 2a.

**Path C -- Diagnosing flat messaging:** If the user says their content "feels generic," "sounds like everyone else," or "isn't landing," check for WHY fuzziness. Signs: leading with features, competing on specs/price, messaging that could belong to any competitor. The book calls this pattern "When WHY Goes Fuzzy" -- it happens when organizations succeed and gradually lose sight of their original purpose. Ask about the founder's origin story, what the company believed before it got big, what changed. Then proceed through discovery.

### 2a. Discover the WHY

If user cannot articulate a clear WHY (purpose, cause, or belief), guide discovery:

**Use the Contribution/Impact Formula:**
Help users draft their WHY using this structure: "To [contribution] so that [impact]"
- First blank: the action taken (contribution to others)
- Second blank: the change desired in others' lives (impact)
- Example: "To inspire people to do the things that inspire them so that, together, we can change our world"

**Apply the 5 Whys Technique:**
- Ask "Why does this matter?" then dig deeper with each answer
- Continue until reaching a fundamental belief about people, the world, or how things should be
- The WHY comes from looking back (origins, experiences, formative moments), not forward. This is a process of discovery, not invention.

**WHY Validation Checklist:**
- Is it a purpose, cause, or belief (not a result like money/success)?
- Does it explain why the organization/person exists beyond products/services?
- Would it remain true even if the products/services changed completely?
- Does it speak to something bigger than the individual?

**Push Back If Needed:**
If stated WHY sounds like a WHAT in disguise, challenge it:
- "We make the best software" -> Ask: "WHY do you make software at all? What do you believe about the world?"
- "We want to be number one" -> Ask: "WHY does being number one matter? What would that enable?"
- "We help customers succeed" -> Ask: "WHY do you care about their success? What belief drives that?"
- "We want to help people" -> Too generic. Ask: "Help people do what, specifically? What experience made you care about this?"

### 3. Define HOW and WHAT

**HOW (Values/Guiding Principles):**
- Express as verbs, not nouns ("always do the right thing" not "integrity"). Verbs are actionable and guide real decisions; nouns become wall art that nobody follows.
- These are actions taken to realize the belief
- Should be specific enough to guide decisions

**WHAT (Tangible Proof):**
- Products, services, actions, results
- Everything said and done that proves the WHY
- Apply Celery Test: Does this WHAT align with the WHY?

### 4. Apply the Celery Test to Your Draft

Use the Celery Test as a writing filter, not just a decision filter:

**Before Drafting:**
Write the WHY statement at the top of the page. This becomes the filter for every subsequent choice.

**Audit the Draft:**
Review every claim, feature, story, or recommendation. Ask:
- Does it fit with the stated WHY?
- Would someone who knows the WHY predict this choice?
- Does it serve as tangible proof of the belief?

**The "Good Advice" Trap:**
Content may be valid, popular, or recommended by others, but if it doesn't align with THIS specific WHY, it's clutter. Remove it even if it's "good."

**The Glance Test:**
Ask a peer to review the draft. Can they articulate the WHY solely from the content included? If they see a disorganized jumble or can't identify the belief, remove more items.

If content fails the Celery Test, flag it to the user.

### 5. Draft Content

**Structure:** Weave Golden Circle naturally. Don't use explicit WHY/HOW/WHAT labels in the output, because the structure should feel like a natural narrative, not an academic framework. Labeling it breaks the spell.
- Open with belief or cause (the WHY)
- Show how values bring it to life (the HOW)
- Present tangible proof (the WHAT)

**Create belonging, not just agreement.** The goal of inside-out content is not just to communicate a belief, but to give readers something to belong to. The book shows that 250,000 people came to hear MLK speak not for him, but for themselves -- his belief aligned with theirs. Ask: does this content make like-minded people feel seen?

**Write for believers first.** Per the Law of Diffusion, your content must resonate with the ~16% who already share the belief (innovators and early adopters) before it can reach the majority. Content that tries to appeal to everyone appeals to no one. Speak to those who already feel what you feel; they will spread the message to the rest. See `references/diffusion-and-tipping-points.md` for more detail.

**Adapt to audience:**
- Executives and entrepreneurs respond to WHY-heavy content (they think in terms of purpose and vision)
- Technical audiences need more HOW (they want to see how the belief translates to practice)
- General consumers need the WHY to be implicit, woven into story rather than stated directly
- Mixed audiences: lead with WHY, quickly move to HOW, let WHAT serve as proof

**Tone:** Default to inspirational and authentic unless user requests otherwise. Authenticity here means the Golden Circle is in balance: what you say and do aligns with what you believe. The limbic brain detects this alignment as "trust."

**Length:** Ask user about intended use and optimize:
- LinkedIn posts: ~150-300 words + relevant hashtags per SEO best practices
- Articles: Ask target word count (typically 800-2000 words)
- Speeches: Ask duration target (150 words ~ 1 minute spoken)
- Other formats: Clarify expectations before drafting

### 6. Avoid Manipulation Tactics

Flag and discourage these approaches (get user approval before including):

| Manipulation | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|--------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Price | Leading with cost savings, discounts | Creates commodity, no loyalty |
| Fear | Warning of consequences if they don't act | Motivates avoidance, not belief |
| Aspiration | Promising dreams without substance | Creates short-term action only |
| Peer Pressure | "Everyone else is doing it" | Appeals to insecurity |
| Novelty | Leading with new features/innovation | Generates interest, not loyalty |
| Promotion | Free stuff, limited time offers | Transactions, not relationships |

If user requests manipulative content, explain: "This approach may work short-term but won't build loyalty. The book shows manipulation creates stress for both buyer and seller. Would you like to find an inspiring angle instead?"

### 7. Review and Feedback

After delivering draft, provide Golden Circle assessment:
- Does the opening establish WHY before WHAT?
- Is the belief clear enough that readers could predict what comes next?
- Do the HOWs feel like natural expressions of the WHY?
- Does every WHAT serve as proof of the belief?
- Would this pass the Celery Test?
- Does it create a sense of belonging, or just deliver information?

Offer to revise based on feedback, but wait for user input before making changes.

## Use Case Examples

### Use Case 1: LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post
**Trigger:** "Write a LinkedIn post about why our consulting firm exists"
**Discovery:** WHY = "To help organizations see what they can't see about themselves"
**Output:** ~250-word post opening with the belief ("Most organizations already have the answers they need -- they just can't see them from the inside"), showing HOW (asking uncomfortable questions, challenging assumptions, holding up mirrors), then WHAT (consulting engagements, strategy workshops). Closes with an invitation to belong: a question or statement that like-minded readers will identify with.

### Use Case 2: Keynote Opening
**Trigger:** "Help me open my conference keynote about AI in healthcare"
**Discovery:** WHY = "To give every patient the care they'd want for their own family"
**Output:** 2-minute opening (~300 words) that starts with the belief, not with AI features. Might open with a personal story about a family member's care experience, then connect it to a broader belief about what healthcare should be. AI enters the narrative as a HOW, not as the headline.

### Use Case 3: Diagnosing Flat Messaging
**Trigger:** "Our website copy feels generic, help me fix it"
**Discovery:** Current copy leads with WHAT (features, pricing, "industry-leading solutions"). Help user uncover their WHY through origin-story questions. Restructure the messaging to lead with belief, moving features into supporting proof.

## Troubleshooting

### User can't articulate a WHY
This is the most common challenge. Don't accept vague answers like "we want to help people" or "we want to be the best" -- these are HOWs or WHATs disguised as WHYs. Use the 5 Whys technique more aggressively. If still stuck, ask about the founder's origin story: "What experience made you start this? What were you frustrated by? What did you believe the world needed?"

### WHY sounds like a mission statement
Mission statements are often aspirational and forward-looking. The WHY comes from looking backward at origins and formative experiences. Redirect: "That sounds like where you want to go. Tell me about where you came from -- what made you care about this in the first place?"

### User insists on manipulation tactics
Explain the short-term vs. long-term tradeoff. If they still want it, comply but flag it clearly: "This will drive transactions but not loyalty. Here's the version you asked for, and here's an alternative that leads with belief instead."

### Content feels formulaic
If the output reads like a template (belief statement, then values, then products), the structure is showing through. Rewrite with the Golden Circle as invisible scaffolding: vary sentence length, use stories instead of declarations, let the WHY emerge rather than stating it upfront. The reader should feel the belief without being told "here is our belief."

### User's organization has lost its WHY
This is the "WHY Goes Fuzzy" pattern from the book. Common in mature companies. Help the user trace back to the founder's original purpose. Ask: "What did this company believe before it got big? What would the founder say if they walked in today?" The WHY doesn't change -- it gets buried. The job is excavation, not invention.

## Output Guidelines

- Never include explicit "WHY/HOW/WHAT" labels in deliverables. The structure should feel like a natural narrative; labeling it makes the content feel like a textbook exercise rather than genuine communication.
- Inform user of Golden Circle structure outside the output (in conversation). This helps them understand the method without it bleeding into the actual content.
- Use book examples internally to shape approach; do not cite them in outputs. The user's content should stand on its own belief, not lean on Apple or Southwest as crutches.
- Ask about length requirements in context of intended platform.
- Default audience: business professionals with tacit subject knowledge.

## Examples of Inside-Out vs Outside-In

**Outside-In (typical, uninspiring):**
> We make great computers. They're beautifully designed, simple to use and user-friendly. Want to buy one?

**Inside-Out (inspiring):**
> Everything we do, we believe in challenging the status quo. We believe in thinking differently. The way we challenge the status quo is by making our products beautifully designed, simple to use and user-friendly. And we happen to make great computers. Want to buy one?

The difference: The second version leads with belief. It gives people something to belong to. The products become proof of the belief, not the reason for the conversation.
